## 1. Clarify First: What Exactly Are the Differences Between Sanyo and Sanin? Why Plan Them Together?

Many people think of "Sanyo/Sanin" as administrative regions at first, but you can actually view them as **two worlds on either side of the same mountain range**:

- **Sanyo (Setouchi / Seto Inland Sea side)**: Hiroshima, Okayama, Onomichi, Kurashiki...  
  The main transportation is the **Sanyo Shinkansen**, featuring higher city density and relatively simple travel.

- **San'in (Japan Sea side)**: Shimane, Tottori (Matsue, Izumo, Yonago, Tottori City...)  
  Attractions are spread out with fewer services, but it offers a stronger vibe of "sea breeze, mythology, hot springs, and sand dunes."

The biggest advantage of combining both is:  
You can first build the "transportation backbone" with Sanyo (Shinkansen, major cities), then extend to San'in using the cross-mountain limited express/bus, which greatly improves time efficiency.

## 2. Transportation Strategy Summary: How to Plan Hiroshima and Okayama Efficiently?

### 1) How to Choose Your Entry Point?

For travelers from Taiwan, common arrival points are typically:

- **Okayama Airport (OKJ)**: A small airport with quick customs, ideal for entering via Okayama, then proceeding to Hiroshima/Kurashiki, and finally extending to San'in.

- **Hiroshima Airport (HIJ)**: Located farther from the city; flight schedules and times may not always be convenient.

- **Fukuoka/Kansai Entry and Exit**: Transportation is easy but requires additional Shinkansen or long-distance transfers.

**Enter and exit via Okayama with convenient timing; then use JR to travel within Sanyo.** This strategy is especially friendly for those who want to explore both Sanyo and San'in in one trip.

![Arrival at Okayama Airport (Screenshot from actual trip)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*09NEVdiOQ3PePi_cmthisA.jpeg)

### 2) How to Choose Your Accommodation Base?

Think of "accommodation" as your transit hub. For the Sanyo side, I would suggest it like this:

- **Hiroshima (Stay 2–4 nights)**: Miyajima, Kure City, and Onomichi (or Fukuyama/Tomonoura) can all be visited as day trips or combined along the route.

- **Kurashiki or Okayama (Stay 1–2 nights)**: Kurashiki is ideal for walking and street photography; Okayama is convenient for train travel, visiting Kibitsu Shrine, and heading north to connect to San'in.

![Hiroshima Accommodation Options Illustration (Original Screenshot)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*-YwLmHr8op_lrrQY3SCd2g.png)

### 3) Where should you connect to San'in?

Conclusion first: **The most reliable place to connect to San'in is "Okayama"**.  
The reason is simple: Okayama offers many limited express trains heading north across the mountains (to Shimane and Tottori), making the route natural and convenient.

### A. Miyajima (Itsukushima Shrine): The Faith and Aesthetics of the Floating Shrine

The most charming aspect of Miyajima (Itsukushima) is not just the floating torii gate, but how it turns the entire island into a "sacred realm":  
The shrine's corridors, torii gate, tides, forests, and deer are all sights you encounter within the same walk.

![View of the torii gate from the shore (less crowded angle)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*DA0LQI4M6jJ9mtd54QF0AA.jpeg)

#### 1) How to Get There (Departing from Hiroshima City)

The most common practice is:

1. Taking the JR/train from Hiroshima to the Miyajimaguchi area

2. Transfer to the ferry to the island (short trip, very smooth)

If you use certain JR passes/tickets, **there is a chance they include the "JR Miyajima Ferry"** (subject to the rules of the ticket you purchased).  
Also, Miyajima has recently added a small "visitor tax" fee, and staff on site usually guide you on how to purchase/pay it.

#### 2) Must-Visit Route in Miyajima (Recommended Half-Day to Full Day)

If this is your first time visiting, I recommend following this order (very easy):

- **Port → Shopping Street Supply → Itsukushima Shrine (Sea Corridor)**

- **Next, go uphill: Ropeway → Shishiiwa Observatory (around Misen)**

- **Take your time exploring and eating oysters/maple leaf-shaped buns on the way back**

![The "floating on the sea" feeling of Itsukushima Shrine's sea corridor](/assets/ai/sanin/1*4UzgS1ocwWemFhgzR4QMVA.jpeg)

##### Key Tip: Watch the Tides (Low tide and high tide feel like two different worlds)

The two most iconic scenes of Miyajima:

- **High Tide**: The sea lifts the shrine, making it look like the entire shrine is floating on the water.

- **Low Tide**: You can walk close to the torii gate (following local rules and safety zones)

Please refer to the tide table of the day for the actual low/high tide times; if you want to capture the scene of walking under the torii gate, **check the tide schedule first before planning your time**, which will save you a lot of luck.

##### Key Activities: Cable Car + Observation Deck (Seto Inland Sea View)

The cable car usually has two sections. The advantage of going up early is "fewer crowds."  
Once you reach the top, the view changes from the "shrine" to the "archipelago": the small islands of the Seto Inland Sea look like scattered puzzle pieces, which is very soothing.

![Cable car and climbing route (original screenshot)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*FuEoMnObeAs0VV-JlHH79A.png)

![Aerial View of the Seto Inland Sea Islands](/assets/ai/sanin/1*2a0jL62PaUm0P_6Dby_rtA.jpeg)

##### Key Highlight: Oysters (Hiroshima Specialty)

If it's your first time visiting Miyajima/Hiroshima, oysters are a must-try ritual. Oyster rice and fried oysters are great options for beginners.

![Miyajima Oyster Cuisine (Travel Record)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*Mpal1Z5DohC1I0-1E9Hv3g.jpeg)

##### A small reminder: Deer are cute, but they also steal food well

The deer on Miyajima are friendly but may bite paper bags, plastic bags, or even your map.  
The best practice is: **Keep food out of sight and immediately put garbage in your bag**.

![Miyajima Deer (Do not feed, watch your belongings)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*eI3Qu90djLf97kfhyHM7XA.jpeg)

### B. Atomic Bomb Dome / Peace Memorial Park: The Most Somber and Essential Part of the Trip

Around Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park, you can treat it as a visit "not just for checking in, but for understanding."  
If you have time, it is also highly recommended to include the Peace Memorial Museum (it can be emotionally heavy, so allow some mental preparation).

![Atomic Bomb Dome (Hiroshima Peace Memorial / Genbaku Dome)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*GFTu9nxh5cOyAO0CJXsRWg.jpeg)

You will encounter many Japanese school field trips, with teachers explaining history to the children and guiding them quietly around. This way of "embedding history into education" helps you better understand why this city chooses "peace" as its core message today.

![Peace Memorial Park Area (Autumn Foliage)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*vff3Syr6WJbE-w9Kr-ngNg.jpeg)

![Evening dome atmosphere (recommended to walk slowly, don't rush the itinerary)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*q90I2dLKoA4V4dAoRGNdqg.jpeg)

- It is recommended to schedule this during a "mentally stable" half-day, avoiding times right before catching a train or flight.

- The museum contains a lot of information, so if you are easily affected emotionally, consider taking a riverside walk or visiting a café to relax afterward.

- Respect the atmosphere: speak softly and avoid exaggerated poses in front of memorial sites.

### C. Walking Around Hiroshima City: Castle Town, Gardens, and "Hiroshima-style Okonomiyaki"

If your time is limited, I recommend focusing on just three things in Hiroshima city:

1. **Hiroshima Castle**: Learn the basic layout of a "castle town"

2. **Shukkeien Garden**: Experience Japanese aesthetics quickly in one garden

3. **Hiroshima-style Okonomiyaki**: Remember the city with one meal

#### 1) Hiroshima Castle: The Starting Point of the City from the Sengoku Era to Modern Times

Hiroshima Castle is a typical "castle town starting point." The original castle was damaged in the atomic bombing at the end of World War II, and most of the current structures are reconstructions.  
Even if you don't enter the main keep, the surrounding moat, plaza, and pathways are great for understanding where Hiroshima "grew from" as a city.

![Hiroshima Castle (Symbol of the Castle Town)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*yxTWLBwpKuf025kiuWemxw.jpeg)

#### 2) Shukkei-en Garden: A Small Garden Capturing All Four Seasons

Shukkeien Garden is a "small but exquisite" garden: the layers of bridges, ponds, trees, stones, and borrowed scenery are all well balanced.  
If you want to fit a "quiet, beautiful, and pleasant to walk" spot into your Hiroshima itinerary, Shukkeien Garden is a perfect choice.

![Shukkeien Garden (Pond View and Small Bridge)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*IGVtKqLpYGJgSZoneGO2aQ.jpeg)

![Shukkeien Garden (Autumn Colors)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*12kJrZFZk-YYZVD7H34Q8A.jpeg)

#### 3) Hiroshima-style Okonomiyaki: Savoring the "Layers" into Memory

Many people can't tell the difference between Okonomiyaki and Hiroshima-style Okonomiyaki. The simplest difference is:

- **Okonomiyaki**: Like mixing ingredients into the batter and then frying

- **Hiroshima-style Okonomiyaki**: Like layering batter, cabbage, noodles, and egg separately

For your first time, it's recommended to go near the station or places like the "Okonomiyaki Gathering Building," where there are many options and it's less likely to be a bad experience.

![Hiroshima Okonomiyaki Story (Around Station Square, Original Screenshot)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*mIjh2c9lqbNQm1dug1HcrA.png)

### D. Kure City: Naval Port Culture, Shipbuilding Industry, and the Story of "Yamato"

Kure City has a clear identity: **If you are interested in modern history, the navy, or the shipbuilding industry, Kure will fully satisfy you.**  
It's more than just museums; walking along the harbor, you can truly feel that "this is a city shaped by the navy."

#### 1) Yamato Museum: Condensing Modern Japan's Shipbuilding and War History into One Museum

The Yamato Museum is the kind of museum where you can spend a long time.  
The exhibits are very detailed, especially the Yamato battleship model and the industrial/military sections. It's easy to lose track of time and stay until closing.

![Kure City: Yamato Museum](/assets/ai/sanin/1*E3npgh-TZU54G4E5JtN1jg.jpeg)

#### 2) JMSDF Kure Museum: Submarines Right Before Your Eyes

The most memorable feature of the Kure Museum is the "actual submarine." Even if you're not particularly interested in military matters, you'll still be amazed by its sheer size.

![Kure City: JMSDF Kure Museum (Submarine Exterior)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*c1BkFzWE_wr4tscbWg8hzQ.jpeg)

**Itinerary Suggestions**

- For an in-depth experience: Spend a whole day in Kure City (including a walk along the harbor)

- For a brief visit: At least half a day is recommended to avoid rushing through both museums.

### E. Onomichi and Tomonoura: The "Slopes" and "Port Town" of the Seto Inland Sea

If Miyajima is the "sacred land" and the Peace Park is the "memory," then Onomichi and Tomonoura represent the "ancient texture of daily life":  
You will see slopes, temples, old streets, ports, and boats waiting for the tide—these are the everyday scenes of the Seto Inland Sea.

#### 1) Onomichi: Senkoji Temple Viewpoint, Temple Town Walk, (Optional) Shimanami Kaido

The classic way to enjoy Onomichi is to "go up the hill for the view, then down the hill for shopping."

- Going up the mountain: walk or take the cable car to the area around Senkoji Temple (to see the city view and the Inland Sea)

- Descent: Cat Alley, shopping street, find a small café to rest

![Onomichi: Senkoji Temple Viewpoint (Cityscape + Onomichi Channel)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*wWrh70gToooi2TxjDmlhCw.jpeg)

![Onomichi: The atmosphere of Cat Alley (store opening hours vary by season)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*KVJtGO9a6ic9xwy_b7oVgA.jpeg)

> If you prefer cycling: Onomichi is also one of the starting points of the Shimanami Kaido, so you can extend your trip into a "Seto Inland Sea cycling day."  
> However, if it's your first visit and you have limited time, just fully explore Senkoji Temple and the old streets—it’s well worth it.

#### 2) Tomonoura: A Historic Port Waiting for the Tide and Wind

The charm of Tomonoura lies in its "smallness," and it is small in a very tasteful way:  
the harbor, the lighthouse, the alleys, and old houses are perfect for a slow stroll, letting time flow naturally.

![Tomonoura: Port Town Entrance and Streetscape](/assets/ai/sanin/1*ZafEophTKy92TAq14VGdYg.jpeg)

Several "historical keywords" about Tomonoura are spot on: it was an important port and is often mentioned in connection with historical figures like Sakamoto Ryoma.  
Even if you're not a history buff, you'll be touched by its "old-fashioned daily life."

![Tomonoura: Sunset time (photographers' favorite time)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*SeorqAWQs7qe8YLMkXwdPA.jpeg)

#### 3) Senzu Island: If You Want a "Quieter Seto Inland Sea"

Walking to Senzu Island leaves a very genuine feeling: it can be quite deserted in the off-season, but the sea and wind are very pure.  
If you enjoy trips with "no people, only the sound of the sea," Senzu Island is a perfect little detour.

### B. Okayama Korakuen + Okayama Castle: A Famous Garden Connected to a Castle

Many Japanese cities separate the "castle" and the "garden," but Okayama has a great rhythm:  
Korakuen Garden and Okayama Castle are very close, so you can think of it as a route of "garden first, then castle."

I happened to visit during Korakuen's illumination season, which greatly enhanced the nighttime atmosphere. Even if you don't come during the illumination period, Korakuen is still worth visiting during the day.

![Okayama: City Movement and Riverside Area (Original Photo)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*RQYpuTgv3MH7d4uaNC4wTw.jpeg)

![Walking atmosphere around Korakuen/Okayama Castle (sunset time)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*21vfIVECQYAq1wHWYx7Vsw.jpeg)

#### 1) Korakuen Garden: Mastering the Art of "Borrowed Scenery"

Korakuen Garden belongs to the type of garden that "looks spacious at first glance but reveals more layers as you walk":  
Your sight is continuously drawn by the grass, ponds, water reflections, trees, and distant views. This is a typical Japanese garden aesthetic: **you walk inside, but your eyes always see farther away**.

![Okayama Korakuen Illumination (Night Atmosphere)](/assets/ai/sanin/1*SxS7Ve_wHlBk57gwA-WsBQ.jpeg)

> [Official Info: Korakuen Garden + Okayama Castle often have illumination events in mid to late November every year (actual dates announced annually)](https://www.okayama-japan.jp/tw/event/19541){:target="_blank"}

#### 2) Okayama Castle: Night View of the Crow Castle and Imagining the Castle Town

Okayama Castle is known as "Crow Castle" (its exterior is dark). The night lighting combined with the surrounding maple leaves creates a great atmosphere.  
If you want to pick just one time slot, **I recommend late afternoon to evening**: first take a walk in Korakuen Garden or along the riverbank, then head to the castle to enjoy the night view. It’s a beautiful way to end the day.

Visiting Kibi-tsu Shrine is perfect for a "cultural finale" because, unlike busy shopping streets, it uses architecture, corridors, trees, and light to help you slow down your pace.

## 6. Seasonal Recommendations: When Is the Most Comfortable Time to Visit?

For a November trip, the experience is very close to "my most recommended season":

- **Spring (March–April)**: Cherry blossoms + comfortable weather (accommodation may be expensive, book early)

- **Autumn (October–November)**: Autumn leaves + dry weather, perfect for walking tours

- **Summer (July–September)**: The Seto Inland Sea is beautiful, but it's hot and humid, so be sure to protect yourself from the sun and stay hydrated.

- **Winter (Dec–Feb)**: Fewer crowds, more light displays, but the sea breeze feels cold (especially in port towns)
