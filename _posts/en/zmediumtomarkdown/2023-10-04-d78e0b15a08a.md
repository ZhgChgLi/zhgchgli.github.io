After arriving in Hakata, I first went to the manned counter to exchange my JR Pass (showing my passport) and reserved a seat for the trip to Nagasaki. There were many foreigners exchanging JR Passes, and I waited for nearly an hour, so I recommend leaving early or exchanging it in advance.

> **_The JR Kyushu Pass can currently only be exchanged at manual counters. Please arrive early to queue for the exchange. If there are too many people at Hakata Station, you can [consider exchanging at nearby stations, which will be faster!](https://www.jrkyushu.co.jp/chinese/railpass/how_to_purchase/reception_place.html){:target="_blank"}_** 

> I bought a five-day pass, which is valid for five days starting from the day of exchange. You can enter and exit the station using this pass (the one with the date & amount), and the reserved seat ticket is just to let you know where your seat is; it cannot be used to enter or exit the station. Keep this pass safe, as you will need it for these five days. If you lose it, it's gone!

There are two segments from Hakata to Nagasaki. You will first arrive at Takeo Onsen and then transfer to Nagasaki from there. You can change trains on the same platform, and they have calculated the train schedules, so basically, after arriving, just walk directly across to board the train.

#### **Additional Notes on Riding Buses and Trams in Japan**
- Ticket number (with a number on it) = There is a small machine at the entrance where you can take a ticket (similar to a number drawing).
- Some routes have a flat fare and may not require a ticket.
- If you use electronic payment (Suica), you can skip taking a ticket, but be careful not to let the balance go negative ( **unlike Taiwan**).
- Buses and trams do not give change, but you can exchange money at the change machine on the vehicle (also near where you put money in for the driver).
- Generally, you board from the back and exit from the front.
- Japanese buses wait for passengers; they will only depart once everyone is seated and will wait for passengers to get off before leaving. So, you can stand up only when the bus arrives at your stop, no need to crowd to the front before reaching your stop ( **unlike Taiwan**).
